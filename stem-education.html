The Importance of S.T.E.M Education

A science, technology, engineering, and mathematics ("STEM") education has become increasingly important in the twenty-first century, where the modern economy demands high skilled workers proficient in quantitative analysis, and where the problems confronting an increasingly globalized world require innovative and creative solutions. From creating new medicines to heal, designing the latest apps to reduce society's transaction costs, to constructing modern high-speed rail to more quickly connect people, a STEM education unites imagination and practicality for the benefit of both individuals and societies. Nations increasingly understand this demand for STEM skills. As of 2011, roughly 41% and 26% of all the degrees awarded in China and India, respectively, were STEM degrees. And in the United States, official statistics estimate that biomedical engineers, medical scientists, software developers, and computer systems analysts - all STEM jobs-are projected to have job increases of 62%, 36%, 32%, and 22% between 2010 and 2020. Comparatively in that same period, all United States occupations are expected to grow only by 14%.

A STEM education creates a supply of skilled workers to meet this demand. As one study notes,"A global labour market is already here, but we lack the institutions to make it work. The real problem for the world economy is not a global shortage of STEM skills, but the location mismatch between employers and employees. Talent cannot move to where the jobs are." Nowhere is this mismatch more pronounced than in rural areas and amongst young women. A STEM education provides the fundamental means to address the mismatch: it provides these rural women the means to become educated and attend a local university, to find employers and use resources by which they can match themselves with employers, and thus contribute to their own well-being, that of their families, and that of their communities.

A STEM education opens the door to the future, not only for individuals to pursue sophisticated and rewarding careers, but also for societies to solve their most pressing issues.
